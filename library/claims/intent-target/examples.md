# Examples

## Example Concrete Claim

- `Pass S1 was intended to reduce ambiguity in the onboarding wiki for new
  engineers trying to complete first-day setup, and success meant one clear
  path with explicit prerequisites, steps, and follow-up questions.`

## Example Supporting Observations

- `plan.md` names new engineers as the primary audience and first-day setup as
  the primary task.
- The pass added prerequisites, ordered setup steps, and a troubleshooting
  section aligned to that task.
- The pass did not attempt broader onboarding redesign outside first-day setup.

## Example Raw Artifacts

- `plan.md` intent section
- onboarding action receipts
- `report.md` execution summary

## Example Verdict

- `supported` because the intended confusion reduction, audience, task, and
  success target were explicit and the applied changes aligned with them.

## Example Open Question Or Follow-Up

- Confirm whether contractor onboarding should remain a non-goal or become a
  separate pass target.

## Anti-Patterns

### Common Failures

- The claim repeats “improve clarity” without naming what confusion is being
  reduced.
- The audience is omitted, so later readers cannot judge relevance.
- The pass target changes during execution but the evidence thread is not
  updated.
- The claim treats the final edits as proof of the target instead of preserving
  the target itself.

### Non-Examples

- Bad claim: `This pass improved the onboarding docs.`
- Why it is bad: it does not say for whom, for what task, or how success should
  be judged.

- Bad observation set: `The docs look cleaner now.`
- Why it is bad: it does not show that the work aligned with the intended
  confusion reduction.
